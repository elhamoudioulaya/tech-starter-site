Step 3

The basics, explained simply

Short, plain-English explainers for the concepts that come up constantly, but rarely get explained clearly.

🔌 Hardware

CPU = how fast it thinks, RAM = short-term memory, storage = long-term memory. Most "slow computer" complaints are actually a RAM or storage problem, not a CPU one.

🖥️ Operating Systems

Windows Event Viewer, macOS Console, and Linux's journalctl are all the same idea: a black-box recorder you check before you guess.

📶 Networking

Most "the internet is broken" issues are DNS or DHCP, not "the internet." Check: connected? → got an IP? → can resolve a website name?

⌨️ Terminal

You need about 15 commands, not a manual. Start with pwd, ls, cd, ping, and ssh — the rest builds from there.

🎫 Helpdesk skills

Reproduce the issue, isolate one variable at a time, and write the ticket for future-you. Technical skill gets you hired; documentation habits get you trusted.

📈 Career basics

Certs open doors, but a documented project or home lab proves you can actually do the work — bring both to interviews.

Step 5

Build a portfolio — step by step

A resume tells people what you claim you can do. A portfolio shows them. It's often the single biggest lever for a junior profile with limited "real" work experience.

WHY 01

It replaces "trust me"

Anyone can list "Python" on a resume. A working project proves you can actually use it.

WHY 02

It's your interview cheat sheet

Interviewers love asking about real projects — it turns the interview into a conversation you're prepared for, not a quiz.

WHY 03

It counts even with zero jobs

Recruiters filtering junior candidates expect thin work history. A portfolio is the thing that's supposed to fill that gap.

WHY 04

It tracks your own growth

Looking back at project #1 vs. project #5 is genuinely motivating on the hard days.

1

Pick a home for it

Don't overthink this. A single GitHub Pages site (like this one!) or a free Notion page both work. What matters is that it's live at one link you can share.

pages.github.com ↗
2

Choose 3–4 projects that show range

Not your ten biggest projects — your most different ones. One that shows you can build a UI, one that shows logic/backend thinking, one that solves a real problem you personally had.

3

Write a short case study for each

For every project, answer three things in a few sentences each: what problem it solved, what tools you used, and one thing you'd do differently now. That last part matters more than people think — it shows judgment, not just output.

4

Add a real "About" section

Skip the generic bio. Say what you're learning right now, and what kind of role you're looking for. Specificity is more memorable than "passionate about technology."

5

Make yourself easy to contact

Email, LinkedIn, and GitHub links, visible without scrolling or clicking through. A recruiter who has to hunt for how to reach you often just won't.

6

Get one honest review before you share it widely

Send it to one person — a mentor, a peer, a community member — and ask what's confusing. You're too close to your own work to catch everything.

7

Treat it as never "finished"

Swap out your oldest, weakest project every time you finish something better. A portfolio is a living snapshot of your current best, not a permanent monument.

a portfolio with 3 solid projects beats one with 10 half-finished ones, every time
