Step 2

The questions you're too embarrassed to ask

Nobody asks these out loud, so here they are, answered plainly.

Do I need a degree to work in tech?
No, not for most entry-level roles, especially in IT support, helpdesk, and increasingly in development. What matters more is demonstrable skill — a portfolio, certs, or hands-on projects. A degree helps in some companies and countries, but it's not the only door in.
How long until I'm "job-ready"?
There's no fixed number, but many people land an entry-level role within 6-12 months of consistent, focused effort — not casual browsing, actual building. Progress compounds: the first project is the slowest, the fifth is much faster.
Is [language/tool] already outdated? Should I even bother learning it?
Almost never as dead as social media makes it sound. Widely-used languages and tools (Python, JavaScript, SQL, Linux fundamentals) stay relevant for years. Fundamentals transfer between tools far more than people starting out realize.
I feel like I'm behind everyone else. Am I?
Almost certainly not — you're comparing your day 1 to someone else's year 3 highlight reel. Everyone you'll meet in tech had a moment exactly like this one.
Should I learn everything, or specialize early?
Generalize first. A few months of broad exposure (see the Start Here tracks) helps you choose a specialty based on real experience instead of a guess.
What if I ask a question and it turns out to be really basic?
It will happen, and it's fine. The engineers who look most confident answering basic questions are usually the ones who got comfortable asking them early and often.

Step 4

The basics, explained simply

Short, plain-English explainers for the concepts that come up constantly, but rarely get explained clearly.

🔌 Hardware

CPU = how fast it thinks, RAM = short-term memory, storage = long-term memory. Most "slow computer" complaints are actually a RAM or storage problem, not a CPU one.

🖥️ Operating Systems

Windows Event Viewer, macOS Console, and Linux's journalctl are all the same idea: a black-box recorder you check before you guess.

📶 Networking

Most "the internet is broken" issues are DNS or DHCP, not "the internet." Check: connected? → got an IP? → can resolve a website name?

⌨️ Terminal

You need about 15 commands, not a manual. Start with pwd, ls, cd, ping, and ssh — the rest builds from there.

🎫 Helpdesk skills

Reproduce the issue, isolate one variable at a time, and write the ticket for future-you. Technical skill gets you hired; documentation habits get you trusted.

📈 Career basics

Certs open doors, but a documented project or home lab proves you can actually do the work — bring both to interviews.

Step 5

The glossary nobody hands you

Terms that get dropped into conversations constantly, on the assumption everyone already knows them. Now you do.

API
A defined way for two pieces of software to talk to each other — like a menu of requests one program can make to another.
Repo (repository)
A folder tracked by Git, containing a project's code and its full history of changes.
Commit
A saved snapshot of your code at a point in time, with a message describing what changed.
Push / Pull
Push sends your local commits to a remote repo (like GitHub). Pull brings down changes made by others.
Branch
A separate line of work off the main codebase, so you can experiment without touching the working version.
Deploy
Taking code from your machine and making it live/accessible — like publishing this site to GitHub Pages.
Staging vs Production
Staging is a practice environment that mirrors the real thing. Production is what actual users see.
Bug
Anything the code does that it wasn't supposed to do. Not a personal failure — just the next thing to fix.
Debugging
The process of finding out why something's broken, usually by narrowing down where it stops behaving as expected.
Stack
The combination of tools/technologies used to build something (e.g. "the stack" for a website might be React + Node + PostgreSQL).
Front-end / Back-end
Front-end is what users see and click. Back-end is the server, database, and logic behind the scenes.
Cloud
Renting computing power/storage from a provider (AWS, Azure, GCP) instead of running your own physical servers.
Container
A lightweight, self-contained package of an app and everything it needs to run, so it behaves the same anywhere (Docker is the most common tool).
Sprint
A short, fixed period (often 1-2 weeks) a team works in before reviewing progress — a common Agile/Scrum practice.
Ticket
A tracked unit of work — a bug report, feature request, or support issue — usually logged in a tool like Jira.
Latency
The delay between an action and its response — high latency is why a page feels "laggy."
Uptime
The percentage of time a system is running and available, as opposed to down or broken.
SSH
A secure way to remotely access and control another computer's command line over a network.
Step 6

Stuck on what to build? Start here.

"Pick a project" is easy to say and hard to do at 11pm with a blank screen. Here are real starter ideas, grouped by track.

🧰 IT Support / Helpdesk

Home network map

Document every device on your home network, its IP, and what it's for — then write up how you'd troubleshoot each one going offline.

IT troubleshooting flowchart

Build a decision-tree diagram for a common issue (e.g. "no internet") — great for showing structured thinking in interviews.

Personal ticket log

Track every tech problem you personally solve for a month, formatted like real support tickets, symptoms → fix.

💻 Software Development

Habit tracker

A simple app to check off daily habits — touches on state, storage, and UI basics without needing a backend.

Personal expense tracker

Log spending by category with basic totals and charts — a genuinely useful tool you'll actually keep using.

A clone of something small

Rebuild a simplified version of an app you use daily (a to-do list, a timer, a notes app) — cloning teaches you to read intent, not just syntax.

☁️ Cloud / SysAdmin

Deploy a static site

Host this kind of site on a free-tier cloud bucket (S3, Azure Blob) instead of GitHub Pages — same result, different pipeline.

Automated backup script

A script that backs up a folder on a schedule — introduces cron jobs / task scheduling, a real sysadmin skill.

Home lab server

Set up a spare machine (or a VM) running a small self-hosted app, like a Pi-hole or a personal wiki.

🔐 Cybersecurity

Password strength checker

A small tool that scores password strength and explains why — teaches you the actual reasoning behind "good" passwords.

Phishing email breakdown

Annotate 3-5 real phishing emails (safely, from public examples) explaining exactly what gives each one away.

Personal home network audit

Use a tool like Wireshark to capture and explain your own home network traffic for 10 minutes — write up what you found.

Step 7

Build a portfolio — step by step

A resume tells people what you claim you can do. A portfolio shows them. It's often the single biggest lever for a junior profile with limited "real" work experience.

WHY 01

It replaces "trust me"

Anyone can list "Python" on a resume. A working project proves you can actually use it.

WHY 02

It's your interview cheat sheet

Interviewers love asking about real projects — it turns the interview into a conversation you're prepared for, not a quiz.

WHY 03

It counts even with zero jobs

Recruiters filtering junior candidates expect thin work history. A portfolio is the thing that's supposed to fill that gap.

WHY 04

It tracks your own growth

Looking back at project #1 vs. project #5 is genuinely motivating on the hard days.

1

Pick a home for it

Don't overthink this. A single GitHub Pages site (like this one!) or a free Notion page both work. What matters is that it's live at one link you can share.

pages.github.com ↗
2

Choose 3–4 projects that show range

Not your ten biggest projects — your most different ones. One that shows you can build a UI, one that shows logic/backend thinking, one that solves a real problem you personally had.

3

Write a short case study for each

For every project, answer three things in a few sentences each: what problem it solved, what tools you used, and one thing you'd do differently now. That last part matters more than people think — it shows judgment, not just output.

4

Add a real "About" section

Skip the generic bio. Say what you're learning right now, and what kind of role you're looking for. Specificity is more memorable than "passionate about technology."

5

Make yourself easy to contact

Email, LinkedIn, and GitHub links, visible without scrolling or clicking through. A recruiter who has to hunt for how to reach you often just won't.

6

Get one honest review before you share it widely

Send it to one person — a mentor, a peer, a community member — and ask what's confusing. You're too close to your own work to catch everything.

7

Treat it as never "finished"

Swap out your oldest, weakest project every time you finish something better. A portfolio is a living snapshot of your current best, not a permanent monument.

a portfolio with 3 solid projects beats one with 10 half-finished ones, every time
Step 8

Write a resume with "no experience"

Most junior resumes fail before anyone judges the content — they're trying to sound like a senior's resume instead of an honest, well-organized junior one. Here's a structure that actually works.

1

Lead with a short summary, not an objective

Two lines on what you're focused on learning and what kind of role you want — skip "seeking a challenging opportunity," it says nothing.

2

Put Projects above Experience

If your strongest proof of skill is a project, not a job, lead with it. Resumes don't have to follow a fixed order — lead with your strongest section.

3

Describe projects by outcome, not just tech used

Weak: "Built a habit tracker using React." Stronger: "Built and deployed a habit tracker used daily, handling state across 4 views." Say what it does and that it's real and live.

4

Reframe non-tech experience honestly

Retail, service, or admin work usually has real transferable skills — troubleshooting under pressure, documentation, working a queue of requests. Name them plainly, don't force awkward tech metaphors.

5

List tools you can actually speak to

A skills section with 25 buzzwords invites questions you can't answer. List what you can genuinely discuss for 2 minutes if asked.

6

Keep it to one page

As a junior candidate, one tight page reads as focused. Two thin pages read as padded.

7

Link your portfolio at the top

Not buried in a footer — right under your name, next to your email. It's often the first link a recruiter actually clicks.

"no experience" almost always means "no job title yet" — you have more proof than you think
Step 9

Interview prep basics

You won't be asked to recite documentation from memory — most junior interviews are checking whether you can reason clearly and explain your thinking. A few common questions per track, plus the behavioral ones that catch people off guard.

🧰 IT Support / Helpdesk
Q.A user says "the internet is down." What do you check first?
Walk through your own troubleshooting order out loud — cable/wifi connected, has an IP, can resolve a domain name. Interviewers are grading your process, not just the final answer.
Q.What's the difference between a process and a service?
A process is running right now; a service is configured to start automatically. Being able to explain this plainly shows real OS understanding.
Q.Describe a time you fixed something you didn't fully understand at first.
Structure it as: what you observed, what you tried, what actually fixed it, and what you learned — this format works for almost any "tell me about a time" question.
💻 Software Development
Q.Walk me through a project on your portfolio.
Have a 60-90 second version ready for each project: the problem, your approach, one technical decision, and one thing you'd change now.
Q.What's the difference between front-end and back-end?
Front-end is what the user sees and interacts with; back-end is the server, database, and logic behind it. Simple, correct answers beat over-explained ones.
Q.How do you approach debugging something you've never seen before?
Reproduce it, isolate one variable at a time, check logs/error messages first before guessing — the same instinct from the Helpdesk guide applies directly here.
☁️ Cloud / SysAdmin
Q.What's the difference between scaling up and scaling out?
Scaling up means a bigger/more powerful single machine; scaling out means adding more machines to share the load.
Q.Why would you use a container instead of a full virtual machine?
Containers are lighter and faster to start since they share the host OS kernel, while VMs run a full separate OS each.
Q.Describe a time something you set up broke unexpectedly.
Focus on what monitoring or symptom told you something was wrong, and what you changed afterward to catch it faster next time.
🔐 Cybersecurity
Q.What makes a password "strong," really?
Length matters more than complexity rules — a long passphrase beats a short password with symbols. Be ready to explain why, not just state it.
Q.How would you recognize a phishing email?
Mismatched sender domains, urgency/pressure language, and links that don't match their displayed text are the classic tells — mention specifics, not just "it looked suspicious."
Q.What's the difference between authentication and authorization?
Authentication proves who you are (logging in); authorization determines what you're allowed to do once you're in.
🗣️ Behavioral (every track)
Q.Tell me about a time you disagreed with someone on a project.
Show that you can hold a technical opinion and still collaborate — the resolution matters more than who was "right."
Q.How do you handle not knowing the answer to something?
"I say I don't know, then go find out" is a genuinely strong answer — confidently faking it is the actual red flag interviewers are listening for.
Q.Why this role, and why now?
Tie it to something specific and recent — a project you built, a track you're focused on — generic enthusiasm is forgettable.
you're not expected to know everything — you're expected to think out loud clearly
Step 10

Real talk

The honest parts nobody puts in the tutorial.

The job market is genuinely competitive right now

Entry-level tech hiring has been tighter the last couple of years than the "learn to code, get rich" era implied. That doesn't mean it's closed — it means volume matters: more applications, more projects, more visible effort than you might expect. Consistency beats intensity here.

Rejection is data, not a verdict

Most junior applicants get far more no's than yes's before their first offer — that's true even for people who go on to have solid careers. One rejection tells you almost nothing about your ceiling; a pattern across many might tell you what to adjust.

Imposter syndrome doesn't fully go away — it just gets quieter

Most people carry some version of "do I actually belong here" well into their careers. The goal isn't to eliminate the feeling before you start; it's to keep going while it's still there.

Your first role won't be your last, and it doesn't have to be perfect

It's tempting to hold out for the "right" first job. A decent first role that gets you real experience is usually worth more than months of waiting for an ideal one that may not exist yet.

Comparing your pace to others online is a losing game

Loud success stories are overrepresented online; quiet, slower progress is not, even though it's far more common. Your timeline is allowed to look different.

slow progress you keep making beats fast progress you give up on
